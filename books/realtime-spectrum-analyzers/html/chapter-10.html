Chapter 10

RTSA Form Factors, Packaging, and Environmental Engineering

The same physics fits in a benchtop chassis, a USB stick, an aircraft pod, and a backpack. The difference is what each form factor costs you, both in dollars and in capability.

10.1 Benchtop Laboratory Systems

The classical RTSA form factor is a benchtop instrument: a chassis with a built-in display, knobs, an RF input on the front, and a power cord on the back. Examples weigh 30 to 60 pounds and cost six figures. They offer the cleanest LO phase noise (large enclosures hold OCXOs and isolation easily), the widest RTBW (full ATX power supplies feed wideband ADCs without thermal limits), the largest displays (10-inch touchscreens are standard), the richest connectivity, and the longest calibration intervals.

The cost is mobility. A benchtop instrument lives on a bench. You bring signals to it through coax cables; you don't bring it to the signals. Right for laboratory R&D, factory production test, compliance certification, defense research, and education.

10.2 Rack-Mounted and Centralized Monitoring

The next form factor up the integration ladder: a 19-inch rack-mount chassis that drops into a network operations center, a cellular site equipment cabinet, or a security monitoring rack. Rack-mount RTSAs typically lack a built-in display and ship with redundant power supplies, hot-swap fans, and remote management interfaces.

Use cases include cellular tower spectrum monitoring, airport spectrum monitoring, stadium and venue spectrum management, and military command-and-control centers. The deployment model is "set and forget." Once installed, the rack-mount RTSA runs unattended for years.

Aaronia's SPECTRAN family fits this role through rack-mount adapters and the cascading architecture: multiple V6 PLUS units cascaded together provide more bandwidth and DF capability than a single rack-mount instrument from any competitor, at a substantial cost advantage.

10.3 OEM Embedded and Sensor-Node RTSAs

When a customer wants RTSA capability inside their own product (an autonomous vehicle, a counter-drone system, a satellite ground station), they need an OEM module: bare circuit boards or compact enclosures that integrate into a larger system without their own user interface.

Characteristics: small physical size (often less than 1 cubic foot), low power consumption (10 to 100 watts typical), standard interfaces (Ethernet, PCIe, USB), programmatic control (no front-panel buttons; everything via API), and reduced feature set tuned for one mission.

Aaronia ships OEM versions of SPECTRAN V6 PLUS for system integrators in counter-UAS, EW, and spectrum monitoring. The hardware is the same as the benchtop USB version, but the software development kit and integration support are tuned for embedded use.

10.4 Portable Field RTSAs

Field engineers need an RTSA they can carry to a problem. Portable RTSAs have battery operation (2 to 6 hours typical), compact size (laptop-sized or smaller), built-in display and controls, rugged enclosures, and outdoor-readable displays.

Use cases include cellular site optimization, intermittent interference hunting, broadcast facility monitoring, and counter-surveillance work where the engineer needs to walk into a building or vehicle and start measuring within minutes.

The Aaronia SPECTRAN V6 PLUS USB form factor (250XA, 500XA, 2000XA) qualifies for this category when paired with a rugged laptop. The unit is USB-bus-powered, fits in a small case, and runs the full RTSA Suite PRO on the host laptop. Cost-effective compared to traditional handheld field analyzers, with substantially more capability per dollar.

10.5 Ruggedized and Military-Grade Systems

The hardest deployment environment is military or harsh-industrial: vibration, temperature extremes, EMI, shock, salt spray, sandstorm, altitude. Ruggedized RTSAs meet specifications like MIL-STD-810 (environmental), MIL-STD-461 (EMI/EMC), and DO-160 (avionics).

Characteristics: conduction-cooled (no fans drawing in dust or moisture), MIL-spec connectors (locked, sealed, shock-resistant), wide temperature operation (-40 to +75 C typical), vibration and shock isolation, EMI containment.

The cost is significant. A ruggedized variant can cost two to three times the equivalent commercial unit. The benefit is operation in conditions where commercial gear simply fails. Aaronia provides ruggedized variants of SPECTRAN through specialized integration partners; the underlying RTSA technology is the same.

10.6 Software Ecosystems

A modern RTSA is half hardware and half software. The user-visible difference between a great RTSA and a mediocre one is often the software.

RTSA Suite PRO

Aaronia's RTSA Suite PRO is the user-facing software for the SPECTRAN V6 PLUS family. It runs on Windows, Linux, and macOS. Key capabilities: real-time spectrum analysis with multiple display modes (waterfall, persistence, density, wrapped spectrum), modulation analysis covering BPSK through 4096-QAM and OFDM, PDW extraction and EW analysis, direction finding via IsoLOG 3D DF integration, 24/7 streaming I/Q recording up to 245 MHz bandwidth, SigMF file format support, visual graph editor for custom processing pipelines, editable measurement presets, and KML / GeoJSON / NMEA / MQTT export.

The visual graph editor is the heart. Users without programming background build sophisticated pipelines by dragging blocks and connecting them. Programmers extend the system by writing custom blocks in Python or C++.

APIs and Automation

For programmatic control, RTSA Suite PRO exposes APIs in Python, C++, and SCPI/VISA. Common automation patterns include factory test scripts, continuous monitoring with custom alerts, and data pipeline integration (stream I/Q to S3 or HDFS for downstream ML training).

Python Ecosystem Integration

The Python ecosystem (numpy, scipy, scikit-rf, GNU Radio, scikit-learn) is the modern lingua franca of RF analysis. RTSA Suite PRO integrates by writing SigMF files that any Python tool reads, by exposing Python API blocks that run inside the graph editor, and by streaming live I/Q to host Python code via shared memory or socket interfaces.

10.7 Why Packaging Matters

Packaging is not just aesthetics. It determines where the instrument can go (benchtop in a lab, rack-mount in a datacenter, portable in a vehicle, OEM embedded in a customer system, ruggedized in a forward operating base), what customers buy (the same RTSA technology in different packaging serves entirely different markets at different price points), total cost of ownership (benchtop instruments cost more upfront but last longer; portable units cost less upfront but require more frequent replacement), and deployment economics.

Packaging also affects calibration intervals. Benchtop instruments in stable lab environments hold calibration for years. Portable units in field environments may need recalibration every 6 to 12 months. Ruggedized military units in extreme environments may need recalibration after every major deployment.

Aaronia in Practice: Scaling Up

A typical SPECTRAN V6 PLUS deployment scales like this. Start with one PLUS 250XA for early-stage exploration. Add a second unit for cross-bearing DF or extended RTBW. Migrate to PLUS 2000XA when the application demands wider bandwidth. Eventually deploy multiple cascaded 2000XA units for full-spectrum surveillance.
